Source: `tmp/tpms/tpms_drive_log.csv` and `journalctl -u tpms-logger.service`, analyzed through
2026-07-19 00:06 MDT. Times below are local MDT. The source CSV is gitignored and continues to
grow; this file preserves the evidence and deductions that matter.

Coverage caveat: the PCAN/interface bring-up failed repeatedly from Jul 16 18:30 until the service
successfully configured `can0` at 21:26. Logged drive coverage resumes at 21:31. The evidence dates
the first **captured** onset to 22:31:47; it cannot exclude an earlier onset during that gap.

Across all 6,891 rows in the CSV through Jul 19 00:06:53, all **708** rows containing an impossible
pressure/sentinel are slot 4, and every one of those rows has `550331=8F`. There are zero invalid
rows for FL, FR, or physical RR, and zero disagreements between slot-4 invalidity and the active
DTC.

`550331` decodes as **C1503-31**: the nominal OEM description is tire-pressure sensor rear left,
no signal. Status `8F` has the current-failure and warning-indicator-requested bits set; after a
valid message arrives it becomes `0E`, then later ages to confirmed-only `08`.

## Deductions

1. This is a real RF-Hub sensor-data loss, not a failed UDS request or PCAN/logger dropout. A failed
   UDS request would leave a blank CSV field; `FFFF` is an explicit invalid value returned by the
   RF Hub, and the hub independently changes the matching DTC status on the same poll.
2. The failing channel is now identified: sensor ID `7004C287`, physically rear left. The evidence
   establishes that the RF Hub intermittently stops receiving usable data for that sensor. It does
   not by itself distinguish a transmitter that stops sending from a receiver/RF-path failure that
   affects only that sensor. A failing transmitter is the leading explanation, but its physical
   manufacturer and age are unknown: the owner reports that two missing sensors were replaced by
   Discount Tire in 2024, and a programmable aftermarket sensor can clone an OEM ID still stored in
   the RF Hub. The `7004xxxx` ID pattern is therefore suggestive of an original ID, not proof that
   the sensor body is OEM or factory-installed.
3. The two relapse intervals of 20m26s and 20m36s closely match the factory diagnostic rule: set a
   wheel fault after a cumulative 20 minutes above 15 mph without a sensor message. A successful
   packet clears the active/lamp state, but another roughly 20-minute receive drought sets it again.
   Provenance for the threshold and warning-display behavior is the local 2022 ProMaster OEM
   C1503-07 RF Hub chart under
   `~/dev/ram_2022_GAS/vehicle/all_diagnostic_trouble_codes_(_dtc_)/testing_and_inspection/c_code_charts/c1503/`.
4. The other three sensor channels remain healthy throughout, while B1040-64 remains merely stored
   and C1512-88 remains absent. That argues strongly against a C-CAN/logger failure and against a
   hub-wide TPMS outage. B1040 is not temporally coupled to this dropout.
5. The fault recurred despite 10-second diagnostic polling and after the four-tire deflate/re-inflate
   exercise. Those interventions may have delayed recurrence, but they did not prevent it.
6. C1503's nominal rear-left label now agrees with the failed sensor's physical rear-left location.
   The old C1504 history and July 7 fault-linked records still show that positions can be wrong, but
   the rear-position mirror should not be treated as a permanent current mapping. A successful
   localization around the time C1512 self-erased on July 13 is plausible, not yet proven.

An off-bus 433 MHz capture (`rtl_433`) or a direct sensor check with a compatible TPMS trigger tool
is the remaining way to split "sensor did not transmit" from "hub did not receive." If choosing a
repair from the present evidence without that extra split, physical-RL sensor `7004C287` is the
evidence-backed replacement target; rewriting unchanged sensor IDs is not a cure for a no-signal
fault.
